# LinkedIn Content Planning

## When to use this skill
- The user wants to create a LinkedIn content plan around a broad topic.
- The user wants content pillars, topic clusters, or subtopic branches for LinkedIn.
- The user has one domain or theme and wants many content angles from it.
- The user wants a repeatable content strategy rather than one-off post ideas.
- The user wants the plan shaped by audience psychology, platform behavior, and current discussion patterns.

## What this skill does
This skill helps the agent convert one broad LinkedIn topic into a structured content planning system.

It does this by:
- Asking the user for the broad topic or area of focus.
- Understanding who the content is meant for.
- Researching how the topic is discussed across the web and social platforms.
- Identifying recurring questions, pain points, debates, myths, and aspirations around the topic.
- Grouping those patterns into content pillars and topic clusters.
- Expanding clusters into branches of subtopics suitable for LinkedIn posts.
- Recommending a content mix and publishing logic for the plan.

## Core principle
The goal is not to generate random post ideas.

The goal is to build a system where:
- The topic is broad enough to sustain recurring posts.
- The subtopics are specific enough to feel fresh and relevant.
- The plan reflects real audience interest, not just generic brainstorming.
- The output can be reused for weeks or months of LinkedIn content.

## Required starting question
The first question to ask is:

- What is the broad topic you want to build LinkedIn content around?

If needed, follow with:

- Who is this content for? (Check `knowledge_company/positioning/icp.md` for existing ICPs).
- Are you trying to build authority, engagement, leads, or personal brand visibility?
- Is this topic tied to your work, product, expertise, or audience interest? (Refer to `knowledge_company/product/features.md`).
- Do you want the plan to lean more educational, strategic, opinion-led, or story-led? (Refer to `knowledge_company/brand/brand_voice.md`).

Ask only the minimum necessary to begin.

## Workflow

### PLAN: Understand the topic and planning context
Build a working brief with these elements:

**0. Research Knowledge Base**
Before asking the user, check the `knowledge_company/` directory for existing context:
- `brand/brand_voice.md`: Tone and personality.
- `positioning/icp.md`: Target audience profiles.
- `positioning/value_propositions.md`: Key benefits and differentiators.
- `product/features.md`: Product capabilities.

1. Broad topic
- What is the main area of focus?
- Example types: AI for sales, grocery shopper behavior, founder branding, B2B partnerships, brand messaging, LinkedIn strategy.

2. Audience
- Who is the intended reader?
- Job role, industry, level, and context if known.

3. Planning objective
- Authority building
- Engagement
- Reach
- Leads
- Personal branding
- Category education

4. Topic fit
Check whether the topic is:
- Broad enough for multiple branches
- Specific enough to attract a meaningful audience
- Relevant to the user’s positioning or long-term goals

If the topic is too broad, narrow it.
If the topic is too narrow, widen it to a more sustainable theme.

### RESEARCH: Expand the topic using public conversation signals
After identifying the broad topic, research how the topic appears in real public discussion.

Use:
- Web search
- Industry blogs
- Forums
- Reddit discussions
- LinkedIn discussions when available
- Social and community discussions when relevant
- News, trend summaries, and recurring audience questions

Research for:
- Common beginner questions
- Advanced questions
- Misconceptions
- Repeated frustrations
- Contrarian opinions
- Tactical how-to needs
- Emerging trends
- Strong debates
- Emotional triggers around the topic
- Use cases and real-world applications

The purpose of research is to surface what people are already thinking, struggling with, asking, and reacting to.

### ORGANIZE: Build content pillars
Turn the research into 3 to 5 major content pillars.

Each pillar should:
- Represent a meaningful branch of the main topic
- Be relevant to the target audience
- Support many future post ideas
- Connect to a clear area of interest, pain, or aspiration

Each pillar should be distinct.
Avoid overlap unless necessary.

### EXPAND: Build topic clusters and subtopic branches
For each pillar, generate topic clusters.

Each cluster should include:
- Core angle
- Why it matters to the audience
- 5 to 15 subtopics or post branches

Good branch types include:
- Mistakes
- Myths
- How-to posts
- Frameworks
- Contrarian views
- Case studies
- Trends
- Stories
- Questions the audience is already asking
- Decision-making criteria
- Before-and-after transformations
- Lessons from practice
- Common objections
- Tool or workflow breakdowns

The purpose is to create a branching map, not a flat list.

### VALIDATE: Check strategic strength
Before finalizing the plan, verify:

- The broad topic can support repeated LinkedIn posting.
- The pillars are distinct and relevant.
- The clusters reflect actual audience interest and not only internal assumptions.
- The branches are varied enough to prevent repetitive content.
- The plan includes both audience-interest topics and authority-building topics.
- The plan is practical for real content creation.

If the plan feels too generic:
- Add more specificity.
- Increase audience relevance.
- Use better research signals.
- Break broad branches into narrower sub-branches.

### EXECUTE: Deliver the content plan
Return the final output in this order:

1. Topic summary
```text
Broad topic:
Audience:
Planning objective:
Planning logic:
```

2. Content pillars
List 3 to 5 pillars with a brief explanation of each.

3. Topic clusters under each pillar
For each pillar, list clusters and explain why they matter.

4. Subtopic branches
Under each cluster, list multiple LinkedIn post branches.

5. Strategic notes
Include:
- Which branches are best for authority
- Which are best for engagement
- Which are best for storytelling
- Which are best for conversion or lead generation

6. Optional planning recommendation
Suggest:
- Which pillar should be used most often
- Which pillar should appear occasionally
- Which cluster types fit educational posts vs story-led posts vs opinion-led posts

## Content planning rules
Follow these rules when building the plan:

- Start from the user’s broad topic, not from arbitrary pillar categories.
- Let audience interest shape the branches.
- Prefer real discussion patterns over generic brainstorms.
- Use a mix of evergreen and timely subtopics.
- Include both high-level and tactical branches.
- Include both rational and emotional angles.
- Make sure the branches can become LinkedIn-native posts, not only blog articles.
- Avoid bland labels like “tips”, “tricks”, or “best practices” unless made specific.

## Pillar design guidelines
Each pillar should answer one of these:
- What part of the topic are people trying to understand?
- What part of the topic are people struggling to apply?
- What part of the topic is changing quickly?
- What part of the topic creates opinions or debates?
- What part of the topic connects most strongly to the user’s authority?

If a pillar cannot support many subtopics, it is too weak.

## Branch design guidelines
A strong branch should be:
- Narrow enough for one post
- Interesting enough to earn engagement
- Useful enough to save
- Relevant enough for the ICP
- Flexible enough to be expressed as:
  - educational
  - storytelling
  - opinion-led
  - framework-led
  - case-study-led

## Recommended output pattern
Use a structure like this:

```text
PILLAR 1: [name]
Why it matters:
- ...

Cluster A:
- Subtopic branch 1
- Subtopic branch 2
- Subtopic branch 3

Cluster B:
- Subtopic branch 1
- Subtopic branch 2
- Subtopic branch 3
```

Repeat for all pillars.

## Content mix guidance
Once the clusters are built, suggest a rough mix of content types such as:
- Educational
- Story-led
- Opinion-led
- Conversion-led

Do not force the same mix for every topic.
Base the recommendation on:
- Audience maturity
- Topic complexity
- Goal of the content plan
- Whether the user is building authority or demand

## Error handling
If the user gives only a vague topic:
- Ask one follow-up question to narrow it slightly.

If the topic is too broad:
- Suggest 2 to 4 narrower positioning options before planning.

If the topic is too narrow:
- Expand to the parent category so the plan becomes sustainable.

If the audience is unknown:
- Infer a likely audience from the topic and state the assumption briefly.

If research signals are weak:
- Build the plan using known audience logic and clear assumptions, then label uncertain areas.

If the branches become repetitive:
- Re-sort them by function: beginner, advanced, emotional, tactical, strategic, controversial.

## Quality checklist
Before finalizing the plan, verify:

- One clear broad topic anchors the full plan.
- The topic has been researched beyond surface-level assumptions.
- The pillars are not repetitive.
- The clusters map to real audience interest.
- The subtopic branches are diverse and actionable.
- The final plan can support multiple weeks of LinkedIn content.
- The output is organized clearly enough for direct use.
